1. Living Building Memory

A system that creates a persistent “memory file” for every building.

Instead of asking an AI to search through hundreds of emails, PDFs, invoices, and letters every time, the AI maintains a continuously updated summary of the property: owners, tenants, open issues, contractors, decisions, obligations, invoices, risks, and recent events.

The key idea: every building becomes a living operational profile.

Why it is interesting: property management is full of repeated context. Every new ticket, email, invoice, or tenant request makes more sense when the AI already knows the building’s history.

Hackathon pitch:
“Every property gets its own AI-maintained memory, so property managers never have to reconstruct context from scattered documents again.”

2. Context Compiler for Real-World Operations

Think of this as a compiler for messy business documents.

Raw inputs are emails, invoices, bank records, scanned letters, tenant messages, meeting notes, and master data. The system turns them into structured operational context that AI agents can reliably use.

The focus is not just search. It is transforming chaotic documents into a usable context layer.

Why it is interesting: most AI document systems retrieve snippets. This idea creates an organized, reusable operating context that gets better over time.

Hackathon pitch:
“We compile messy property-management documents into a clean context layer that AI agents can act on.”

3. Property Timeline Intelligence

A system that builds a chronological story of each property.

For every building, it creates a timeline of important events: tenant complaints, maintenance requests, owner decisions, contractor visits, invoices, payments, legal letters, insurance claims, and follow-ups.

The AI can then answer questions like:

“Why was this invoice issued?”
“What happened before this tenant complained?”
“How long has this issue been open?”
“Did the contractor already respond?”

Why it is interesting: property management problems are rarely isolated. They usually depend on history.

Hackathon pitch:
“We turn scattered property documents into a timeline of what happened, what is unresolved, and what needs attention.”

4. AI Operations Briefing for Property Managers

A daily or weekly briefing system for property managers.

Instead of manually checking emails, invoices, bank data, and documents, the manager gets a concise briefing:

“What changed?”
“What needs action?”
“What is risky?”
“What is waiting on someone else?”
“What should be escalated?”

This could be framed as an executive assistant for property operations.

Why it is interesting: the value is immediately understandable. Property managers are overloaded, and the system helps them prioritize.

Hackathon pitch:
“An AI briefing that tells property managers what changed, what matters, and what to do next.”

5. Source-Backed Decision Layer

A system that helps AI make decisions only when the supporting context is clear.

For example, before suggesting whether an invoice should be approved, the AI checks:

Is this contractor known?
Was work requested?
Was the work completed?
Does the amount match previous invoices?
Is there tenant or owner communication about this issue?
Is the invoice linked to the right building?

The core idea is not simply “summarize documents,” but “make AI decisions traceable and defensible.”

Why it is interesting: Buena’s domain involves real-world actions, money, tenants, owners, and compliance. AI needs reliable context before acting.

Hackathon pitch:
“We make AI actions auditable by grounding every recommendation in the property’s living context.”

6. Noise Filter for Property Documents

A system that decides what information is worth remembering.

Property managers receive many documents: repetitive notifications, irrelevant emails, duplicate attachments, outdated letters, newsletters, small talk, and low-value messages.

This concept focuses on separating signal from noise.

The AI asks:

Does this change the property context?
Does this create a task?
Does this update a known issue?
Does this affect money, risk, tenants, owners, or compliance?
Is it duplicate information?

Why it is interesting: advanced context management is not only about adding information. It is also about knowing what not to add.

Hackathon pitch:
“An AI memory system that knows what to remember, what to ignore, and what to update.”

7. Context-Aware Ticket Assistant

A system that helps resolve tenant or owner requests using building-specific context.

When a new tenant email arrives, the AI does not treat it as a standalone message. It knows the building, previous complaints, contractors, open repairs, recent invoices, owner decisions, and pending tasks.

Example:

Tenant says: “The heating still isn’t working.”

AI understands:

There was a heating complaint last week.
A contractor was contacted.
An invoice was received.
Another tenant reported the same issue.
The property manager has not yet confirmed completion.

Why it is interesting: it shows very clearly why context management matters.

Hackathon pitch:
“Tenant tickets become smarter because the AI understands the full building context before responding.”

8. Building Health Score

A more product-like concept: each property gets a dynamic “health score.”

The score could reflect:

open maintenance issues,
unpaid invoices,
unresolved tenant complaints,
legal or compliance risks,
slow contractor response,
missing documents,
financial anomalies.

The system turns documents into an operational dashboard.

Why it is interesting: it converts context into a simple business signal.

Hackathon pitch:
“We turn property documents into a live health score for every building.”

My top three for a hackathon would be:

Living Building Memory as the core concept,
Property Timeline Intelligence as the demo experience,
and Noise Filter for Property Documents as the advanced context-management angle.
